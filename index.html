Critical findings

🚫
The whole process happened without neighbors knowing
The county purchased the land in 2021, selected an operator in 2024, approved a 20-year lease, and issued construction permits — all without a single public hearing. Community Development Director Ann Lippmann confirmed this in writing on April 20, 2026. Because the property has been zoned industrial since 1969, no rezoning was needed — and without rezoning, no hearing was ever legally required.
⏰
There are no county rules on hours, trucks, or odor
The county applied zero operational conditions during plan review. What time trucks can arrive, where they stage, how odor is managed — none of it is governed by anything Paulding County can enforce. It falls entirely to the private contract with Capital Waste Services and GA EPD state rules, neither of which neighbors have seen.
🚛
No traffic study was done, despite a weight-limit road
The NW Georgia Regional Commission exempted this project from a Development of Regional Impact review — so no traffic study was ever required. Rosedale Drive, the primary truck access road, is a two-lane residential road with a strict 18-ton weight limit. Tractor-trailers will exceed that limit. GDOT is reportedly in discussions but no plan is public.
☣️
The county's own attorney flagged a cancer-chemical risk
At the April 14, 2026 Work Session, the county attorney raised PFAS ("forever chemicals") contamination as a real risk. The county retained the law firm Arnold and Nitkin LLP on contingency to pursue litigation against PFAS manufacturers — an implicit admission that contamination of groundwater is a foreseeable outcome.
🚧
Construction violated a stream buffer and triggered a stop work order
On April 6, 2026, a county inspector issued both a Stop Work Order and a Stream Buffer Violation — meaning work had been done inside the protected buffer zones near the creek. The site was marked "in compliance" the following week, but no corrective action documentation has been made public.
📄
Key documents in the plan set contradict each other
The area of land to be disturbed is listed as 12.24 acres in hydrology and permit documents, and 10.86 acres in clearing and grading submittals — a 1.38-acre gap. This number determines what erosion controls are required and the bonding the county holds. Which figure was used for the Land Disturbance Permit is not publicly confirmed.
📣
Neighbors across two counties are organized and on record
Over 1,660 people signed the Change.org petition. Menllo Station (Paulding) and Warren Creek (Cobb County) residents both spoke publicly. Powder Springs Councilmember Raja Antone formally demanded relocation. Cobb County Commissioner Monique Sheffield is actively working with Paulding officials. The Ozora Road transfer station in Gwinnett County was completely withdrawn after similar opposition — that's a real precedent.

1
Get the two documents that govern everything
All operational rules — hours, truck staging, odor, noise — live in exactly two documents: the Capital Waste Services operating contract and the GA EPD Notification of Permit by Rule (Section 391-3-4-.06(3)(b)). File an Open Records Request under Georgia's Open Records Act (O.C.G.A. § 50-18-70). The county must respond within 3 business days.
Also request:
· Phase 3 Final Erosion Control Plan (and its submission date vs. the Dec 22, 2025 LDP)
· April 6, 2026 Stop Work Order documentation and any GA EPD corrective action
· The official LOD figure used for the LDP (12.24 or 10.86 acres?)
· The NW Georgia Regional Commission DRI exemption letter
